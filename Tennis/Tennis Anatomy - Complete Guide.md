# Tennis Anatomy -- Complete Guide

Based on *Tennis Anatomy* by E. Paul Roetert and Mark S. Kovacs (Human Kinetics, USTA-backed). Adapted for a 38-year-old recreational player with Scheuermann's disease, adductor history, and Achilles sensitivity.

---

## Part 1: How Tennis Uses Your Body

Tennis is not an arm sport. It is a full-body, rotational, multi-directional sport that demands strength, flexibility, power, endurance, and speed all at once. Understanding how your body actually works during a match changes how you train.

### The Kinetic Chain

Every tennis stroke follows the same principle: force starts at the ground and transfers upward through a chain of body segments.

**Ground → Feet → Ankles → Calves → Knees → Quads/Hamstrings → Hips → Core (obliques, abs, back) → Shoulder → Arm → Wrist → Racket → Ball**

This is Newton's third law in action. Your feet push against the court, the court pushes back, and that force travels up through each link. If any link is weak, leaky, or stiff, the chain breaks. You lose power, and the segments above the weak link have to compensate -- which is how injuries happen.

The three critical links most people neglect:
1. **Hips** -- the engine of rotation
2. **Core** -- the transfer station between lower and upper body
3. **Posterior shoulder** -- the braking system that decelerates your arm after every stroke

### The Numbers

- Tennis averages **3-5 direction changes per point**
- A typical match involves **500+ directional changes**
- **60-80% of movement is lateral** (side to side), not forward/backward
- Points last 5-15 seconds on average, but matches can go 2-3 hours
- The serve can generate shoulder internal rotation speeds of **1,074 to 2,300 degrees per second**
- After contact, your posterior shoulder muscles must decelerate all of that rotational velocity eccentrically

This means tennis is simultaneously aerobic (match duration) and anaerobic (short explosive bursts). Training must reflect both.

## Part 2: Stroke Mechanics and Muscle Involvement

### The Forehand

The forehand is the most commonly hit stroke in modern tennis, and the open-stance forehand dominates at every level. It is the most rotational groundstroke.

**Backswing (loading phase):**
- Lower body loads eccentrically: gastrocnemius, soleus, quads, glutes, hip rotators
- Trunk rotates: internal oblique (same side) and external oblique (opposite side) contract concentrically
- Shoulder and arm externally rotate: posterior deltoid, infraspinatus, teres minor, latissimus dorsi contract concentrically
- Front shoulder and chest stretch eccentrically: anterior deltoid, pectoralis major, subscapularis

**Forward swing (acceleration):**
- Lower body drives concentrically and eccentrically: calves, quads, glutes, hip rotators
- Trunk rotates through: obliques, back extensors, erector spinae
- Shoulder accelerates: latissimus dorsi, anterior deltoid, subscapularis, biceps, pectoralis major contract concentrically

**Follow-through (deceleration):**
- Arm decelerates eccentrically: infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid, rhomboids, serratus anterior, trapezius, triceps, wrist extensors

**Key insight:** The muscles that decelerate the forehand (posterior shoulder, upper back) work harder eccentrically than the muscles that accelerate it work concentrically. This is why shoulder injuries come from weak decelerators, not weak accelerators.

### The One-Handed Backhand

Uses less trunk rotation than the forehand but requires more coordinated action between shoulder and forearm rotation. The front leg is more involved. Wrist extensor strength and endurance are critical -- weakness here leads to tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis).

**Key muscles:** Infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid, trapezius (acceleration phase). Subscapularis, pectoralis major, biceps, wrist flexors (deceleration).

### The Two-Handed Backhand

Greater trunk rotation than the one-handed backhand. The obliques are heavily involved, especially in open-stance backhands. The nondominant arm contributes significantly -- its flexors, extensors, and wrist muscles must be trained.

**Key insight:** The two-handed backhand puts enormous rotational demand on the core. If your obliques are weak, your lower back compensates. For someone with Scheuermann's, this makes core training non-negotiable.

### The Serve

The most physically demanding stroke. The serve is a full kinetic chain event that requires every segment to fire in sequence.

**Loading phase:**
- Knees flex eccentrically (quads) to build ground reaction force
- Trunk counter-rotates to store elastic energy
- Shoulder reaches maximal external rotation (~170 degrees): infraspinatus, teres minor, supraspinatus, biceps, serratus anterior all active
- Eccentric loading: subscapularis and pectoralis major stretch

**Acceleration phase:**
- Explosive vertical push: calves, quads, glutes drive concentrically
- Trunk unwinds: abdominals, obliques contract concentrically; back extensors eccentrically
- Shoulder internally rotates at extreme speed: subscapularis, pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, latissimus dorsi, triceps, forearm pronators

**Follow-through:**
- Landing absorption: calves, quads, glutes contract eccentrically (shock absorption)
- Arm deceleration: infraspinatus, teres minor, serratus anterior, trapezius, rhomboids, wrist extensors all contract eccentrically
- This is the phase where most shoulder injuries occur

**Two serve types:**
- **Foot-up serve:** Back foot slides forward to join front foot during toss. More forward momentum, easier hip rotation.
- **Foot-back serve:** Back foot stays in place. More balanced, potentially more vertical force.

### The Volley

Shorter stroke with abbreviated backswing and follow-through. Same muscles as groundstrokes but in smaller ranges of motion. The lower body demand is arguably greater because volleys require:
- Deeper knee flexion (low volleys)
- Faster split steps and direction changes
- Greater eccentric loading in the quads for lunges to the ball

**Key insight:** Good volleys are hit with the feet, not the hands. Leg strength -- especially the ability to lunge deeply and recover quickly -- is the foundation of net play.

## Part 3: Playing Styles and Training Implications

| Style | Movement Pattern | Training Priority |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Serve & Volleyer** | 20-40% more forward movement than baseliners | Quad and calf strength for low volleys, wrist flexibility, functional flexibility for deep lunges |
| **Aggressive Baseliner** | Moves inside the court, takes balls early | Upper body power (chest, front shoulder), explosive hip rotation, core power |
| **Counterpuncher** | 60-80% lateral movement, chases everything | Abductor/adductor strength, muscular endurance (upper and lower), single-leg stability, oblique endurance |
| **All-Court Player** | Mix of baseline and net play | Balance all muscle groups, weight transfer drills, transition movement (approach shots to net) |

Most recreational players are a mix. Your tennis schedule (3x/week, singles and doubles) means you need elements of all four, with emphasis on lateral endurance and rotational power.

## Part 4: Training by Body Region

### Shoulders -- The Most Important and Most Injured Joint

The shoulder is a ball-and-socket joint with the greatest range of motion in the body -- and therefore the least inherent stability. The rotator cuff (four small muscles) is responsible for keeping the humeral head centered in the socket during every explosive stroke.

**The four rotator cuff muscles:**
1. **Supraspinatus** -- initiates arm abduction (lifting arm to the side)
2. **Infraspinatus** -- external rotation (backswing, deceleration)
3. **Teres minor** -- external rotation (works with infraspinatus)
4. **Subscapularis** -- internal rotation (acceleration, forehand forward swing)

**Why tennis players get shoulder injuries:**

The muscles that accelerate the arm (pectoralis major, subscapularis, anterior deltoid) are naturally stronger because they are bigger and used in daily life. The muscles that decelerate the arm (infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid, rhomboids) are smaller and often neglected in training. This creates an imbalance. Over thousands of serves and forehands, the decelerators cannot keep up with the accelerators. The rotator cuff fails.

**Essential shoulder exercises:**

**1. External Rotation with Resistance Band**
- Stand sideways to the band anchor
- Elbow pinned to hip, 90-degree bend
- Rotate forearm away from body against resistance
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps per arm
- *Variation:* Place a rolled towel between elbow and ribs -- increases infraspinatus activation by ~20%
- *Why:* Directly trains the decelerators that protect your shoulder after every stroke

**2. 90/90 External Rotation (Abducted)**
- Face the band anchor, arm at 90 degrees shoulder height, elbow at 90 degrees
- Rotate forearm from parallel to floor to perpendicular
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps per arm
- *Why:* Mimics the exact shoulder position during the loading phase of the serve. Trains deceleration in the sagittal plane

**3. 90/90 Internal Rotation (Abducted)**
- Face away from anchor, same 90/90 position
- Rotate forearm from perpendicular to parallel
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps per arm
- *Why:* Strengthens the accelerators in the serve position. Increases serve speed over time

**4. Front Raise**
- Standing, dumbbells at thighs, palms down
- Raise both arms straight forward to shoulder height, hold 2 seconds
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps, light weight (<5kg)
- *Why:* Anterior deltoid drives forehand acceleration and high-ball forehands

**5. Lateral Raise**
- Standing, dumbbells at sides, palms facing thighs
- Raise both arms out to sides to shoulder height, hold 2 seconds
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps, light weight (<5kg)
- *Why:* Lateral deltoid is critical for one-handed backhand acceleration and deceleration, and serve backswing

**6. Bent-Over Rear Raise**
- Bent at waist, back straight, slight knee bend
- Elbows bent 90 degrees, raise forearms to shoulder height
- Squeeze shoulder blades together at the top
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps, light weight
- *Why:* Posterior deltoid and rhomboids -- the primary decelerators. This exercise directly prevents shoulder injuries

**7. Elbow-to-Hip Scapular Retraction**
- Standing, arms at 90-degree shoulder and elbow angles
- Lower elbows toward hips by contracting rhomboids
- Hold at bottom for 2-4 seconds
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps
- *Why:* Scapular position determines injury risk. Weak scapular stabilizers are the root cause of most shoulder problems in tennis players. Also improves posture -- directly relevant for Scheuermann's

**8. Low Row with Band**
- Face the anchor, arms low, grasp band
- Pull hands back with straight arms, squeezing shoulder blades
- Hold 2 seconds at end range
- 2-3 sets x 10-12 reps
- *Why:* Strengthens lower trapezius, posterior deltoid, and rhomboids together

### Core and Torso -- The Transfer Station

Think of your core as a cylinder: front (rectus abdominis), sides (obliques), and back (erector spinae, multifidus). Tennis demands movement in all three planes:
- **Sagittal plane** (forward/backward): crunches, back extensions
- **Frontal plane** (side to side): side planks, lateral flexion
- **Transverse plane** (rotation): cable rotations, medicine ball throws

The obliques are the most tennis-specific core muscles. Every open-stance forehand and two-handed backhand is powered by oblique rotation. The erector spinae and multifidus protect the spine during rotation -- especially critical with Scheuermann's.

**Essential core exercises:**

**1. Plank (Front)**
- Standard plank on forearms, body straight
- 3 sets x 30-45 seconds (build to 60 seconds)
- *Why:* Anti-extension -- teaches the core to resist sagging, which protects the lumbar spine when your thoracic spine is already compromised

**2. Side Plank**
- On one forearm, body straight, hips stacked
- 3 sets x 20-30 seconds per side
- *Progression:* Add hip dips (10 per side) -- lowers and lifts the hip while maintaining the plank
- *Why:* Oblique endurance and lateral stability. Directly supports every open-stance groundstroke

**3. Dead Bug**
- Lying on back, arms reaching to ceiling, knees at 90 degrees
- Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously, keeping lower back pressed to floor
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Anti-extension under movement. Teaches the core to stabilize while limbs move -- exactly what happens during a stroke

**4. Bird Dog**
- On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg
- Hold 3 seconds at the top, keep hips level
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Anti-rotation and posterior chain activation. Excellent for Scheuermann's because it trains thoracic extension without loading the spine

**5. Cable/Band Rotation (Pallof Press)**
- Stand sideways to cable/band anchor at chest height
- Press arms straight out in front of chest, resist the rotation
- Hold 2-3 seconds, return
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Anti-rotation -- the core learns to resist being pulled into rotation, which is the exact eccentric demand during the deceleration phase of every rotational stroke

**6. Medicine Ball Rotational Throw**
- Stand sideways to a wall, feet shoulder-width apart
- Rotate hips and trunk to throw the ball against the wall
- Catch and repeat
- 3 sets x 8-10 per side
- *Why:* Concentric rotational power. This is the most tennis-specific core exercise because it trains the exact hip-to-trunk-to-arm sequence used in forehands and serves

**7. Farmer's Carry**
- Heavy dumbbells or kettlebells, one in each hand
- Walk 30-40 meters with upright posture, shoulders back
- 3 sets
- *Why:* Full-core activation under load. Trains grip, shoulders, obliques, and erector spinae simultaneously. Also directly useful for Nepal pack carrying

### Legs -- The Foundation

Tennis is a leg sport disguised as a racket sport. Your legs provide the ground reaction force that powers every stroke, the lateral movement to reach every ball, and the endurance to do it for 2-3 hours.

**The muscles that matter most:**
- **Quadriceps** -- knee extension, deceleration after sprints, low volleys
- **Hamstrings** -- hip extension, knee flexion, sprint acceleration
- **Glutes (maximus and medius)** -- hip extension and rotation, lateral stability
- **Gastrocnemius and soleus (calves)** -- push-off, split steps, vertical drive on serves
- **Adductors** -- pulling the leg toward center, critical for recovery after wide steps
- **Abductors (gluteus medius, tensor fasciae latae)** -- pushing the leg away from center, critical for lateral movement

**The 60-80% rule:** Since most tennis movement is lateral, 60-80% of your leg training should involve lateral movements (lateral lunges, lateral band walks, side shuffles). Most people train forward/backward movements almost exclusively and wonder why they are slow getting to wide balls.

**Essential leg exercises:**

**1. Lateral Lunge**
- Step wide to one side, bend the stepping leg, keep the other leg straight
- Push off the bent leg to return to standing
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* The single most tennis-specific leg exercise. Mimics reaching for a wide forehand or backhand. Trains adductors and abductors simultaneously
- *Note for you:* Start with bodyweight only. Your adductor history means you need to build range gradually. If there is any groin discomfort, reduce depth

**2. Forward Lunge**
- Step forward, lower back knee toward the ground, push back to standing
- 3 sets x 10 per leg
- *Why:* Quad, glute, and hamstring development. Mimics the split step and recovery movement

**3. Squat**
- Feet shoulder-width, sit back and down, knees tracking over toes
- 3 sets x 12-15 reps (bodyweight or light weight)
- *Why:* Foundational lower body strength. Trains the quads and glutes concentrically (standing up) and eccentrically (sitting down)

**4. Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift**
- Stand on one leg, hinge at the hip, extend the other leg behind you
- Light dumbbell in opposite hand
- 3 sets x 8-10 per leg
- *Why:* Hamstring and glute strength on a single leg. Improves balance and ankle stability. Trains the posterior chain that protects the lower back during trunk rotation

**5. Calf Raises**
- Standing on a step, rise onto toes, lower below the step level
- 3 sets x 15-20 reps (slow, controlled)
- *Why:* Gastrocnemius and soleus drive every split step and serve push-off. Important for Achilles health -- eccentric calf raises are the gold standard for Achilles tendon conditioning
- *Note for you:* Slow eccentric (lowering) phase is critical. Take 3-4 seconds to lower. This loads the Achilles tendon in a controlled way that builds resilience

**6. Lateral Band Walk**
- Resistance band around ankles, quarter-squat position
- Sidestep for 10-15 steps in each direction
- 3 sets
- *Why:* Gluteus medius activation. The glute med is the primary lateral stabilizer -- when it is weak, your knee collapses inward during lunges and direction changes. This is a common cause of knee pain in tennis

**7. Single-Leg Balance**
- Stand on one foot, eyes open (progress to eyes closed)
- 3 sets x 30 seconds per foot
- *Why:* Proprioception and ankle stability. Reduces ankle sprain risk. Also relevant for Nepal trekking on uneven terrain

### Back -- The Protective Layer

The back muscles (latissimus dorsi, rhomboids, trapezius, erector spinae) serve two roles in tennis:
1. **Power production** -- the lats and traps contribute to serve speed and backhand acceleration
2. **Deceleration and protection** -- the rhomboids, traps, and posterior shoulder decelerate the arm after every stroke

**Essential exercises:**

**1. Bent-Over Row**
- Hinged at hips, back flat, pull dumbbells to ribcage
- 3 sets x 10-12 reps
- *Why:* Rhomboids, lats, traps -- the entire posterior chain. Balances the dominant chest/shoulder muscles from all your forehands and serves

**2. Lat Pulldown or Pull-Up**
- Pull the bar to upper chest, squeeze shoulder blades together at the bottom
- 3 sets x 8-12 reps
- *Why:* Latissimus dorsi power. The lats are a primary accelerator in the serve and overhead

**3. Superman / Back Extension**
- Lying face down, lift chest and legs simultaneously, hold 2-3 seconds
- 3 sets x 10-12 reps
- *Why:* Erector spinae and multifidus. Directly strengthens the muscles that protect the thoracic and lumbar spine during rotation -- critical for Scheuermann's

### Chest -- The Accelerator

The pectoralis major is the primary accelerator of the forehand and serve. It is usually naturally strong in tennis players because it is trained by the sport itself. The risk is overdevelopment relative to the back, which pulls the shoulders forward and increases injury risk.

**Essential exercises:**

**1. Push-Up**
- Standard push-up, full range of motion
- 3 sets x 10-15 reps
- *Why:* Pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, triceps. Functional chest strength that also engages the core. Better than bench press for tennis because the scapulae move freely

**2. Standing Cable/Band Chest Press**
- Press forward against band resistance at chest height
- 3 sets x 10-12 reps per arm
- *Why:* Single-arm chest press mimics the forehand forward swing. Trains the pec in a standing, rotational context

**Rule:** For every chest exercise, do at least one back exercise. The back-to-chest training ratio should be at least 1:1, ideally 1.5:1, to maintain shoulder health.

### Arms and Wrists -- The Final Link

The forearm muscles control the racket. Weak wrists and forearms lead to tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis from wrist extensor overload) and wrist injuries.

**Essential exercises:**

**1. Wrist Curls (Flexion)**
- Forearm on thigh, palm up, curl a light dumbbell
- 3 sets x 15 reps
- *Why:* Wrist flexor strength for serve wrist snap and forehand topspin

**2. Reverse Wrist Curls (Extension)**
- Forearm on thigh, palm down, extend a light dumbbell
- 3 sets x 15 reps
- *Why:* Wrist extensor endurance. These muscles are the ones that fail in tennis elbow. Training them prevents the most common overuse injury in the sport

**3. Forearm Pronation/Supination**
- Hold a hammer or weighted object at the end of the handle
- Rotate forearm palm-down (pronation) and palm-up (supination)
- 3 sets x 10 each direction
- *Why:* Pronation is the final wrist action in the serve. Supination is involved in slice backhands

## Part 5: Rotational Strengthening

Modern tennis is built on rotation. The open-stance forehand and two-handed backhand generate power through the sequential rotation of hips, trunk, and shoulders -- not through arm strength. Players who rely on arm strength plateau quickly and get injured.

**The rotation sequence:** Hips rotate first, creating a stretch across the obliques. The trunk unwinds next, pulling the shoulder. The shoulder pulls the arm. The arm whips the racket. Each segment accelerates the next, like cracking a whip.

**Key rotational exercises:**

**1. Cable/Band Wood Chop (High to Low)**
- Stand sideways to anchor point at head height
- Pull diagonally across body from high to low, rotating hips and trunk
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Mimics the serve follow-through and overhead. Trains the obliques in the diagonal pattern they actually work in during play

**2. Cable/Band Reverse Wood Chop (Low to High)**
- Anchor at knee height
- Pull diagonally from low to high, rotating hips and trunk
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Mimics the forehand forward swing from low to high. Trains the hip-to-trunk rotation sequence

**3. Seated Medicine Ball Rotation**
- Sit on floor, knees bent, feet off ground (V-sit position)
- Hold medicine ball at chest, rotate side to side touching the ball to the ground
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Oblique endurance under fatigue. Simulates the core demand of long rallies

**4. Standing Hip Rotation with Band**
- Band around thighs, athletic stance
- Rotate hips against resistance while keeping shoulders still
- 3 sets x 10 per side
- *Why:* Hip-shoulder separation. The ability to rotate your hips while your shoulders stay loaded is the secret to power in all rotational strokes

## Part 6: Movement Drills

Getting to the ball matters more than what you do when you get there. The best forehand technique is useless if you arrive late, off-balance, or out of position.

**1. Spider Drill**
- Place 5 balls on the court (one on each corner of the service boxes and one at the center T)
- Start at the baseline center
- Sprint to each ball, pick it up, return it to the baseline
- Time yourself -- this tests multi-directional speed and endurance

**2. Split Step Drill**
- Partner feeds balls randomly
- Before each shot, perform a proper split step (small hop, land on balls of feet, ready position)
- Focus on timing: the split step happens as the opponent makes contact

**3. Lateral Shuffle**
- Set two cones 5 meters apart
- Shuffle between them in athletic stance, touch each cone
- 3 sets x 30 seconds
- Focus on staying low, pushing off the outside foot

**4. Cross-Step to Recovery**
- Start at the center mark
- Cross-step to reach a wide ball (right foot crosses over left for forehand side)
- Hit the imaginary ball, then shuffle back to center
- 10 reps per side

## Part 7: Common Tennis Injuries and Prevention

| Injury | Cause | Prevention |
|--------|-------|------------|
| **Rotator cuff tendinitis** | Weak decelerators, overuse of serve | External rotation exercises, back strengthening, 1.5:1 back-to-chest ratio |
| **Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)** | Wrist extensor overload, poor backhand technique | Reverse wrist curls, eccentric wrist extension exercises, check grip size |
| **Lower back pain** | Weak core, excessive rotation without stability | Core cylinder training (front, side, back), avoid pure crunches, bird dogs and dead bugs |
| **Knee pain (patellofemoral)** | Weak glute medius, knee valgus during lunges | Lateral band walks, single-leg squats, balance training |
| **Ankle sprain** | Poor proprioception, weak peroneals | Single-leg balance (eyes closed progression), calf raises, ankle rotations |
| **Adductor strain** | Sudden lateral lunge beyond trained range | Lateral lunges with progressive depth, adductor stretching, Copenhagen plank |
| **Achilles tendinopathy** | Repetitive loading without adequate eccentric training | Eccentric calf raises (slow lowering), gradual volume increase |

## Part 9: Training Principles

### Volume and Intensity

- **Hypertrophy (muscle size):** 8-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 60-80% of max, 30-90 second rest
- **Strength:** 6 reps or fewer, 2-4 sets, 80-100% of max, 2-3 minute rest
- **Endurance:** 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets, below 60% of max, less than 30 second rest

For tennis performance at 38, prioritize **muscular endurance and power** over pure strength. You do not need to squat 150kg. You need your muscles to fire explosively 500+ times per match without fatiguing.

### Frequency

- Train similar muscle groups 2-3x per week
- At least 24 hours between sessions targeting the same muscles
- 2 sessions per week maintains strength; 3 sessions per week builds it

### Rest and Recovery

- 3 minutes for immediate energy stores to replenish after a 10-60 second burst
- Neural recovery (from explosive movements like plyometrics) is harder to feel but equally important
- Do not train through pain. Soreness is normal. Sharp pain is a warning. Joint pain is a stop sign.

### Adaptation and Detraining

- Aerobic fitness is lost faster than strength when you stop training
- Flexibility gains and losses happen rapidly
- Muscle strength maintains for 2-3 weeks without training, then declines
- This means your 3-week Nepal trip will cost some tennis-specific strength. Plan a 1-2 week rebuilding phase when you return
