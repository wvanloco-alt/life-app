# How to Start Winning Tennis Matches

A personalized guide for competition season (May 2026), based on *The Inner Game of Tennis* (Gallwey), *Winning Ugly* (Gilbert), and *Tennis Anatomy* (Roetert & Kovacs). Written for a player with good strokes, strong conditioning, and great ball-chasing ability -- who used to keep losing.

---

## The Honest Diagnosis

You are the textbook "plays beautiful tennis and loses" player. Everyone tells you how well you hit the ball. Your technique is solid. You run down everything. Your opponents complain about how many balls you chase. And then you lose 6-2, 6-1 to someone you know you are better than.

This is not a talent problem. It is a combination of three problems, each reinforced by the others:

1. **You play tennis, but you do not play your opponent.** You hit the ball without a plan. You have no idea where you should be standing, where you should be aiming, or what pattern you are trying to create. You are a beautiful hitter with no strategy.

2. **Your mind takes over on the important points.** You open your body, look at the court instead of the ball, and overhit. These are not technique problems. They are symptoms of Self 1 (your thinking mind) hijacking Self 2 (your body) at precisely the moments when you need to trust your body the most.

3. **You do not know how to convert offense into points.** You recognize short balls, you know you should attack them, but you make errors because you are rushing, not watching the ball, and not finishing the point with a plan.

Each of these is fixable. Not with more lessons on your forehand, but with tactical awareness, mental discipline, and deliberate practice of specific match situations.

## Part 1: The Mental Game -- Why You Are Beating Yourself

### The Soccer Player Problem

You run at the ball. Multiple people have told you this. In soccer, you chase the ball, you close down space, you sprint toward the action. This instinct is deeply grooved into your movement patterns from decades of soccer.

In tennis, the ball comes to you. Your job is to set up, load your weight, and let the ball arrive into your hitting zone. When you run at the ball, several things go wrong simultaneously:

- You arrive too close to it, cramping your swing
- Your weight is moving forward through the shot instead of rotating
- You have no time to set your feet, so you hit off-balance
- The faster the ball comes, the worse this gets -- which is exactly why faster opponents destroy you

**The fix is not to run less.** Your movement is an asset. The fix is to change *what* you run to. Instead of running to the ball, run to the position where you will hit the ball. This sounds like the same thing, but it is fundamentally different. When you run to the ball, you aim for where the ball is. When you run to the hitting position, you aim for where you need to be when the ball arrives -- roughly arm's-length away from it, with your feet set and your weight loaded.

**Practice drill:** During rallies, focus on arriving early enough to take one adjustment step before the ball reaches you. If you are still moving when you hit, you arrived too late -- or you ran too far. The goal is: run fast, arrive early, be still when you hit.

### Why You Suck at Warm-Ups

Gallwey explains this perfectly. During warm-up, you are thinking. You are evaluating your shots. "My forehand feels off today." "I'm not hitting clean." Every judgment creates tension. Every bit of tension degrades your fluidity. You spiral: bad shot → self-criticism → tighter muscles → worse shot → more criticism.

Meanwhile, once the match starts and the competitive instinct kicks in, you stop thinking and start reacting. Self 2 takes over because there's no time for Self 1 to interfere. This is why you play worse in warm-up than in the match -- your conscious mind has too much room to meddle.

**The fix:** Treat the warm-up as observation, not evaluation. Gallwey's technique: watch the seams of the ball. Do not judge whether each shot was good or bad. Just observe where it went. "That ball landed two feet inside the baseline." Not "nice shot" or "come on, hit it cleaner." The more neutral your observation, the faster Self 2 finds its rhythm.

Gilbert adds a tactical layer: use the warm-up to scout your opponent, not to fix your own strokes. Watch where their weaknesses are. Do they like pace or hate it? What does their backhand look like under pressure? Use the warm-up minutes to build your game plan instead of stressing about how your forehand feels.

### Opening Your Body and Overhitting

This is the single most revealing symptom in your list. When you open your body too early -- rotating your hips and shoulders toward the court before contact -- you are doing something very specific: you are looking at where you want the ball to go instead of watching the ball arrive at your racket.

Gallwey calls this Self 1 trying to control the outcome. Your conscious mind wants to see the result before it happens, so it pulls your eyes -- and therefore your head, shoulders, and hips -- toward the target. The kinetic chain breaks. Your racket face opens. The ball sails long.

This gets worse on big points. When the point matters, Self 1 gets more anxious about the outcome, so it looks at the target even earlier. The more you care about the result, the worse your execution becomes.

**The fix is counterintuitive: stop caring where the ball goes.** Not permanently, but in the moment of the shot. Gallwey's technique is to program Self 2 with the target *before* the point starts (visualize where you want to aim), then during the point, focus entirely on the ball. Watch the seams. Watch the felt. See the ball hit your strings. Let Self 2 handle the aiming -- it already knows where you want the ball to go.

The physical cue: keep your non-hitting shoulder pointed at the net until after contact. If your chest opens toward the court before the ball leaves your racket, you opened too early. Practice this in rallies by literally telling yourself "shoulder stays" on every shot. Not as a permanent instruction, but as a temporary anchor to retrain the habit.

### Anger Management -- You're Actually Doing OK

Your description of how you handle anger -- getting mad briefly, then putting a smile on and resetting before the next serve -- is healthy. Gilbert describes two types of psyching: what your opponent does to you, and what you do to yourself. Self-inflicted psyching (dwelling on mistakes, spiraling into frustration) is far more destructive than brief flashes of anger.

Gallwey would add: the smile is good because it interrupts the judgment cycle. When you frown or curse after a miss, you are labeling the shot as "bad," which triggers Self 1 to start analyzing and instructing, which creates tension, which makes the next shot worse. A smile -- even a forced one -- signals to your nervous system that the event is not a threat.

The key is what happens after the smile. Between points, focus on your breathing. Inhale through the nose walking back to position. Exhale slowly. Let the breath bring your mind back to the present. Gallwey recommends this as the single best between-points technique: "the second my mind starts wondering about whether I'm going to win or lose, I bring it gently back to my breath."

## Part 2: The Tactical Game -- How to Actually Win Points

This is the biggest gap in your game. You have the shots. You have the fitness. You have no plan.

### Where to Stand in Singles

Most recreational players stand in the wrong place. Here are the three positions you need to understand, and when to be in each:

**Position 1: The Baseline Rally Position**
Stand about half a meter behind the baseline, roughly in the center of the court. After every shot, recover to this position. Not to the exact center -- adjust slightly toward the side you hit to (if you hit crosscourt to the right, recover slightly right of center), because your opponent's highest-percentage reply will come back roughly to the same area.

**Position 2: The Approach Position**
When you get a short ball (lands inside the service line), you move forward. Hit your approach shot, then continue moving to the net. Your target position is about two meters inside the service line, split-stepping as your opponent hits. The split step is non-negotiable -- it loads your legs for a reaction in either direction.

Where you stand at the net depends on where you hit your approach shot. Imagine a line from your approach shot's landing point to the center of your baseline. Stand on that line. This bisects the angle of your opponent's possible replies. If you hit your approach down the line, you stand toward that side. If you hit it to the middle, you stand roughly central.

**Position 3: The Recovery After a Wide Shot**
When you are pulled wide to hit a ball, you have two choices:
- Hit crosscourt (the safe, high-percentage play) and recover to center
- Hit down the line (the aggressive, riskier play) and move forward to cover the open court

Most of the time, go crosscourt. This gives you more court to hit into (the diagonal is longer than the sideline), more net clearance (the net is lower in the center), and more recovery time. Down the line should be a surprise weapon, not your default.

### Where to Hit: The Three Golden Rules

Gilbert's entire philosophy boils down to this: maximize your strengths, minimize your weaknesses, exploit your opponent's weaknesses, and neutralize their strengths. In practice, for you, that means three rules:

**Rule 1: Hit most balls crosscourt and deep.**
Crosscourt is the highest-percentage shot in tennis. The net is 15cm lower in the center than at the posts. The diagonal is about 1.2 meters longer than the sideline. You have a larger margin for error. Hit crosscourt and deep (past the service line) and you are never in a bad position. This should be your default on 70% of shots.

**Rule 2: Change direction only on short balls.**
When the ball lands short (inside the service line), that is your signal to attack. Step inside the baseline, take the ball early, and hit down the line or into the open court. Do not change direction from the baseline on a deep ball -- the geometry is against you and the risk of error is high.

**Rule 3: Hit to their weakness until they prove they can handle it.**
Play your opponent, not the court. If their backhand is weaker, hit 80% of balls there. Gilbert kept a "little black book" on every opponent. You play the same people regularly. You should know everyone's worst shot. Hit there. Relentlessly. It is not rude. It is tennis.

### The Slice: Your Secret Weapon That You Forget Exists

You say you have a good slice but never think about using it. This is a massive missed opportunity. The slice is the most tactical shot in recreational tennis because it does three things that flat or topspin balls do not:

1. **It stays low.** A well-hit slice skids and stays below the net level, forcing your opponent to hit up. Hitting up means they cannot attack. This neutralizes power players.

2. **It changes the rhythm.** If you have been hitting topspin rallies, a sudden slice breaks the tempo. Your opponent has to adjust their timing, footwork, and swing path. This is devastating against players who rely on rhythm.

3. **It buys you time.** The slice travels slower through the air. When you are stretched wide or off-balance, a deep slice gives you two extra seconds to recover to position.

When to use it:
- As an approach shot (slice approach + move to net is one of the highest-percentage attacking patterns in tennis)
- When you are pulled wide and need time to recover
- Against fast-paced opponents who thrive on pace (take the pace away)
- On the backhand side when you are under pressure
- As a change-up after three or four topspin rallies to break their rhythm

**Practice commitment:** In your next five practice sessions, hit at least 10 slice backhands per session as approach shots, then move to the net behind them.

### Attacking Short Balls Without Making Errors

You know you should attack mid-court balls. You try. You make mistakes. Here is why, and how to fix it:

The mistake is that you treat the approach shot like a winner. You see the short ball, your eyes light up, and you try to blast it into the corner. Self 1 gets excited about the opportunity and tries to do too much. The racket opens, the body rotates too early, and the ball goes long or wide.

An approach shot is not a winner. It is a setup. Its job is to put you in a position to hit an easy volley. The approach shot should be:

- **Deep** (past the service line, ideally into the last third of the court)
- **Placed** (to a weakness, usually down the line or into the body)
- **Not overhit** (75% power, with control and spin)

After the approach, move forward. The volley is where you finish the point.

The technical fix for your specific errors on approach shots: because you tend to open your body and overhit, use the slice approach as your default. The slice naturally keeps your body sideways, keeps the ball low, and prevents overhitting. It is the safest, most effective approach shot in recreational tennis.

**The drill:** Practice the sequence as one continuous movement: split step → recognize short ball → move forward → slice approach down the line → continue to net → split step → volley into open court. Practice this sequence until it becomes automatic. The slice keeps you controlled; the continuous forward movement prevents you from stopping in no-man's land.

### Your Net Game Problem

You make mistakes at the net in singles but play well in doubles. The difference is preparation and positioning:

In doubles, you start at the net. You are already there, set up, ready. Your body is loaded and your eyes are on the ball.

In singles, you approach the net during the point. You are running forward, often arriving rushed and off-balance. You are still in "running mode" when the ball comes, so you swing instead of punching, and you look at the court instead of the ball.

Gallwey's volley principles solve this:

**"Meet the ball as far in front of you as you comfortably can."** In front of you is where you see the ball best, where you have the best angles, and where you have the most power. If you are contacting the ball beside or behind your body, you waited too long or arrived too late.

**"Bite the ball."** Gallwey's Harvard coach Jack Barnaby used to shout this. It means get your face close to the ball. This forces your head forward, your weight forward, and your eyes onto the ball. You said you "almost never look at the ball at the net." This is the core problem. Bite the ball.

**No backswing.** You do not have time for a backswing at the net. Prepare by having your racket in front of you, head up, face slightly open. When the ball comes, punch through it. Do not swing. The shorter the stroke, the more control you have.

**Split step before every volley.** As your opponent hits, do a small hop and land on the balls of your feet. This loads your legs for a push in either direction. Without the split step, you are flat-footed and slow. This is probably the single biggest difference between your doubles net play (where you split step naturally) and your singles net play (where you are still moving forward when the ball comes).

## Part 3: Competition Preparation -- February to May

You have roughly three months. Here is how to use them.

### Phase 1: February-March (Foundation -- you are here)

**Focus: Build tactical habits in practice**

You are currently preparing for Nepal (March 27 - April 16). Tennis during this period should focus on building tactical awareness, not fitness. You already have the conditioning.

In every practice session:
1. Pick one tactical theme (e.g., "hit 70% crosscourt today" or "use slice on every approach")
2. Practice the approach shot → volley sequence at least 10 times
3. Watch the seams of the ball -- build the concentration habit now
4. After every session, write three observations in a notebook: what worked, what your opponent did, what you noticed about your own patterns

### Phase 2: Late April (Post-Nepal -- Sharpening)

**Focus: Match simulation and point play**

When you return from Nepal (around April 17), you have roughly two weeks before competition starts.

- Play practice sets with the explicit goal of implementing your game plan (hit to weakness, approach on short balls, recover to center)
- Practice closing out games. When you are up 40-15, play the next point exactly the same way you played the previous points. Do not change your approach because you are ahead
- Practice receiving serve: stand a step closer than you normally would. Gilbert recommends choosing to receive first in matches. Your return game is already good -- lean into it

### Phase 3: May (Competition Season)

**Pre-match checklist (do this every single time):**

1. On the way to the court, answer two questions:
   - What do I want to make happen? (e.g., "I want to hit 80% of balls to his backhand, approach on short balls, and use my slice to break his rhythm")
   - What do I want to prevent from happening? (e.g., "I do not want to get into fast-paced baseline rallies where I overhit")

2. During warm-up: scout your opponent, do not fix your own game. Watch their backhand. Watch where they stand on returns. Observe, do not judge your own shots.

3. First two games: minimize unforced errors. Get every ball back. Put the ball in play. Do not go for anything. Force your opponent to beat you. Gilbert says: stuff your opponent early. Get out of the gate fast. Win the first game if you can.

4. First changeover (after game 3): ask yourself "who is doing what to whom?" Am I dictating play or reacting? Adjust the plan if needed.

**During points:**
- Watch the seams
- Default crosscourt and deep
- Slice when you need time
- Approach on short balls (slice approach, not flat blast)
- Move to net behind the approach
- Split step. Every. Single. Time.

**Between points:**
- Breathe. Inhale walking back. Exhale slowly
- No verbal self-criticism. Zero. Observe, do not judge
- If you feel anger rising, smile, then breathe. Reset before you serve or return

**When ahead:**
Gilbert's technique: lie to yourself. If you are up 5-3, tell yourself you are down 3-5. This prevents the mental letdown that happens when you start playing "not to lose" instead of playing to win. Do not become conservative. Keep doing what got you ahead.

**When behind:**
Gallwey's technique: forget the score. Play each point as its own event. The score is 0-0 on every point. Focus on executing your plan for this point, not on what happened three games ago.

## Part 5: The Mindset Shift

There is one paragraph in Gallwey that captures exactly what you need to internalize before competition season:

> "Today I play every point to win. It's simple and it's good. I don't worry about winning or losing the match, but whether or not I am making the maximum effort during every point because I realize that that is where the true value lies. Maximum effort does not mean the super-exertion of Self 1. It means concentration, determination and trusting your body to 'let it happen.' It means maximum physical and mental effort."

You said it yourself: "I'm never really thinking about how I can win, I just play." That is the problem and, paradoxically, also close to the solution. You need to start *before* the match with a plan for how to win. But once the point starts, you need to stop thinking and just play -- with your body doing the work while your mind stays quiet, focused on the ball, trusting that the plan will execute itself through your trained instincts.

The goal is not to become a robotic, tactical machine. The goal is to combine your natural, joyful, ball-chasing athleticism with enough tactical structure that all that beautiful effort actually converts into points.

You already have the hardest thing to teach: you love this game, you move well, and you never give up. Now you just need to play smart while doing it.
