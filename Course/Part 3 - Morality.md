# Part 3: Morality — Evolution and Concepts

### Everything I Know — A Course in Understanding Reality
*Based on the collected readings and podcasts of Wim Van Loco*

---

## Preface

We have now traced the story from the Big Bang through the origin of life, the evolution of complexity, and the emergence of a species — Homo sapiens — that can ask questions about its own nature. In Part 2, we explored what drives human behavior: neurons, hormones, genes, development, and culture, all interacting in a temporal cascade.

Now we arrive at what may be the most consequential question of all: How should we behave? What is right? What is wrong? And can we answer these questions with anything more reliable than ancient texts, cultural tradition, or gut feeling?

This part explores morality — not as a set of commandments but as a natural phenomenon. We will trace its evolutionary origins in the behavior of genes and the structure of the brain, examine the philosophical arguments for grounding morality in science rather than religion, and confront the hardest questions: free will, in-group bias, and the gap between moral intuition and moral reasoning.

**Sources for this part:** Sam Harris — *The Moral Landscape*, Christopher Hitchens — *God Is Not Great*, Robert Sapolsky — *Behave*, Richard Dawkins — *The Selfish Gene*, Yuval Noah Harari (Lex Fridman podcast), David Deutsch (Tim Ferriss and Within Reason podcasts), Sam Harris (Tim Ferriss podcasts), Sean Carroll (Lex Fridman podcast), Nassim Nicholas Taleb (Tim Ferriss podcast) — *Skin in the Game*, Russ Roberts (Tim Ferriss podcast) — *Wild Problems*, Derek Sivers (Tim Ferriss podcasts)

## The Evolutionary Roots of Morality

At first glance, morality seems like a problem for evolutionary theory. Natural selection favors genes that help organisms survive and reproduce. A truly "selfish" organism — one that takes without giving, cheats without consequence, and exploits every advantage — should outcompete a moral one. So why do humans have concepts like fairness, guilt, compassion, and justice?

The answer, as we saw in Part 1 through Dawkins, lies in the gene's-eye view. Genes do not "see" individuals; they "see" copies of themselves. A gene that causes an organism to help its relatives — who carry copies of the same gene — will spread. This is kin selection: the evolutionary logic by which altruism toward genetic relatives can be favored, because helping your kin helps your genes propagate. A gene that causes an organism to cooperate with non-relatives, on the condition of future reciprocation, will also spread — as long as cheaters can be detected and punished. This is reciprocal altruism: the tit-for-tat logic of cooperation among strangers, where helping someone today increases the chance they will help you tomorrow.

Robert Trivers proposed that many of our moral emotions evolved specifically to support reciprocal altruism. Gratitude motivates you to repay a favor. Guilt punishes you internally when you fail to reciprocate. Moral outrage motivates you to punish cheaters. Sympathy motivates you to help others in distress, who may later help you. These are not arbitrary feelings. They are evolved mechanisms for maintaining cooperative relationships in a species that lives in social groups and depends on others for survival.

Even envy has a function: it alerts you when someone is getting more than their fair share, motivating you to demand equity. And shame — the pain of being caught violating a social norm — deters behavior that would damage your reputation and reduce the willingness of others to cooperate with you. The moral emotions, in other words, are not luxuries. They are the psychological infrastructure of cooperation.

## The Trolley Problem and Moral Intuition

Sapolsky, in his chapter on morality, uses the famous trolley problem to illustrate the tension between moral intuition and moral reasoning. The thought experiment comes in several versions.

In the first version, a runaway trolley is heading toward five people. You can pull a lever to divert it onto a side track, where it will kill one person. Most people say they would pull the lever. It feels like simple math: one death is better than five.

In the second version, the same trolley is heading toward the same five people. But this time, you are standing on a bridge next to a large man. The only way to stop the trolley is to push him off the bridge onto the tracks. His body will stop the trolley and save the five. Most people say they would not push him — even though the arithmetic is identical.

Why the difference? Because pushing someone to their death engages the limbic system — the emotional brain — in a way that pulling a lever does not. The lever feels abstract, mechanical, distant. The push feels personal, visceral, violent. The utilitarian calculation is the same, but the emotional experience is radically different.

This tells us something important about moral judgment: it is not a single, unified process. It is a negotiation between emotional intuitions, rooted in the limbic system, and rational deliberation, rooted in the prefrontal cortex. Sometimes they agree. When they don't, we experience moral conflict. Sam Harris, in his podcast discussions, leans toward consequentialism — the view that the right action is the one that produces the best outcomes. But he acknowledges that the emotional resistance to pushing the fat man is not irrational. It reflects a deep concern about what kind of person would be willing to do that, and what kind of society would normalize it.

## Sacred Values

Sapolsky notes that certain values become "sacred" — removed from utilitarian calculation entirely. Ask someone how much money it would take for them to sell their child, and they will not just refuse; they will be offended by the question. The very act of treating a sacred value as tradeable feels like a violation.

Sacred values serve an important social function: they create boundaries that protect cooperation. If everything is negotiable, trust collapses. But sacred values also create rigidity. They can prevent compromise even when compromise would reduce suffering. Much of the world's most intractable conflict involves clashes between sacred values on different sides — each side convinced that its non-negotiables are self-evident, and the other side's are barbaric.

## The Moral Landscape

Sam Harris, in *The Moral Landscape*, makes a provocative claim: morality is a domain of facts, and science can — in principle — determine human values.

His argument begins with a thought experiment. Imagine the worst possible misery for everyone — a world in which every conscious being suffers as much as possible, for as long as possible. Harris argues that if you agree this is bad — objectively bad, not just "bad for you" — then you have accepted that morality is about the wellbeing of conscious creatures, and that wellbeing is a factual matter that depends on events in the world and states of the brain.

Once you accept this, the rest follows. If wellbeing depends on brain states, and brain states can be studied scientifically, then there are scientific truths about what increases and decreases wellbeing. Morality is not a matter of opinion or cultural preference. It is an undeveloped branch of science.

Harris visualizes this as a "moral landscape" — a space of possible outcomes where peaks represent states of high wellbeing and valleys represent states of severe suffering. There may be multiple peaks, many different ways to live well, and the landscape may be complex and difficult to map. But the existence of peaks and valleys is a factual matter, not a matter of taste.

---

## Against the Fact-Value Distinction

The traditional philosophical objection to Harris's view comes from David Hume: you cannot derive an "ought" from an "is." The fact that something causes suffering does not, logically, tell you that you ought to avoid it. There is a gap between facts and values.

Harris argues that this gap is illusory. He offers three reasons.

First, wellbeing is a factual domain. Questions about what makes conscious creatures flourish or suffer are questions about the world — about neuroscience, psychology, economics, and social structure. These are amenable to scientific investigation.

Second, objective knowledge itself already involves values. Science values evidence, logical consistency, and parsimony. These are not derived from science — they are the values that make science possible. If we already accept that some ways of thinking about the world are better than others, and we do every time we reject creationism in favor of evolution, then we have already accepted that values and facts are entangled.

Third, Harris points to neuroscience research, including his own fMRI studies, showing that the brain processes factual beliefs and moral beliefs using similar mechanisms. The ventromedial prefrontal cortex is engaged when evaluating both "2+2=4" and "it is wrong to torture children for fun." This does not prove they are the same kind of belief, but it suggests that the sharp line philosophers draw between them may not correspond to how the brain actually works.

## Moral Relativism and Its Problems

Harris is perhaps most forceful in his rejection of moral relativism — the view that moral judgments are valid only relative to a culture, and that no culture's morality is objectively better than another's.

He argues that this position, while it sounds tolerant and open-minded, is actually incoherent. If we discover that a particular cultural practice — say, forcing women to live under cloth bags, or mutilating the genitals of children — causes immense suffering and prevents human flourishing, then calling that practice "just as valid" as any other is not tolerance. It is moral cowardice disguised as sophistication.

The key distinction is this: acknowledging that moral questions are complex and that we may not have all the answers is reasonable. Claiming that there are no answers — that a society built on cruelty, ignorance, and oppression is morally equivalent to one built on knowledge, compassion, and freedom — is absurd.

## The Argument Against Religious Morality

Christopher Hitchens, in *God Is Not Great*, and Sam Harris, in *The Moral Landscape* and his podcasts, both argue that religion is not a reliable source of moral guidance. Their arguments are complementary.

Hitchens identifies four "irreducible objections" to religious faith. First, it misrepresents the origins of humans and the cosmos, offering creation myths where there should be scientific inquiry. Second, it combines maximum servility — submission to God — with maximum solipsism, the belief that the universe was created for you. Third, it is both cause and result of dangerous sexual repression, generating guilt, shame, and the pathological control of human sexuality. Fourth, it is grounded on wish-thinking — believing what you want to be true rather than what the evidence supports.

Hitchens backs these claims with a relentless catalog of religious violence: Belfast, where Protestants and Catholics fought; Beirut, with its sectarian civil war; Bombay and Hindu nationalism; Belgrade, where Orthodox, Catholic, and Muslim identities clashed; Bethlehem, where Hamas imposes Islamic law; Baghdad and the Sunni-Shia divide; the Rushdie fatwa; and 9/11. His conclusion: religion does not just fail to prevent violence; it actively motivates and justifies it.

Harris's critique is more focused. He argues that religious morality is not actually about wellbeing — it is about obedience. The Ten Commandments, for example, contain no prohibition against slavery, no mention of torture, no defense of children. They are primarily concerned with loyalty to God. A moral system that ranks "not worshipping other gods" above "not owning human beings" is, Harris argues, not a serious moral framework.

---

## Can People Be Good Without God?

Both Hitchens and Harris answer: obviously. The claim that morality requires religion is not only false but insulting. It implies that the only thing preventing people from murder and rape is fear of divine punishment — a view that says more about the person making the argument than about humanity.

Harris notes that secular societies — like those in Scandinavia — consistently outperform religious ones on virtually every measure of societal health: lower crime, lower inequality, better education, better healthcare, higher reported happiness. This is not proof that atheism causes wellbeing, but it decisively refutes the claim that religion is necessary for it.

Hitchens puts it memorably: name one moral action or statement made by a believer that could not have been made by a nonbeliever. Now name a wicked action or statement that could only have been made by someone who believed they were acting on God's instructions. The second list, he notes, is very long.

---

## The Problem of Moderation

Harris raises a subtler point about religious moderation. Moderate believers — those who reject literalism and violence — are often held up as proof that religion can be benign. But Harris argues that moderates give cover to fundamentalism by defending the idea that faith itself is a virtue. As long as "believing without evidence" is respected, you cannot effectively criticize those who take that principle to its logical extreme.

The solution, for both Harris and Hitchens, is not moderate religion but secularism — the principle that religion should be kept out of public policy and that moral questions should be addressed through reason, evidence, and honest conversation about human wellbeing.

## The Biology of In-Group Bias

Sapolsky devotes an entire chapter of *Behave* to the Us/Them dichotomy, and it is one of the most important chapters in the book.

Humans automatically categorize people into in-groups and out-groups. This categorization is fast — measurable in milliseconds via amygdala activation. It is unconscious — it happens before the cortex can intervene. And it is extraordinarily flexible: the boundaries of "us" and "them" can shift based on context.

Race is one such category, but far from the only one. Studies show that in-group bias can be triggered by something as trivial as the color of a t-shirt or being randomly assigned to a team. The categorization is not fixed by biology; it is constructed by culture and context.

Dehumanization is the extreme end of this spectrum. When an out-group is described using metaphors of disease, vermin, or contamination — Jews as rats, Tutsis as cockroaches, immigrants as an "invasion" — the brain processes them differently. The medial prefrontal cortex, which is normally activated when thinking about other people's mental states, goes quiet. The out-group is being processed as objects, not persons. Sapolsky's chapter on "Metaphors We Kill By" traces how dehumanizing language enables mass violence. Once people are linguistically reclassified as insects or cancer, killing them becomes not murder but pest control or surgery.

## Oxytocin and the Double Edge

As we discussed in Part 2, oxytocin — often called the "bonding hormone" — is not simply pro-social. It is pro-in-group. It makes you warmer toward your own people and colder toward outsiders. In experiments by De Dreu, Dutch participants given oxytocin were more willing to sacrifice a person named "Ahmed" than a person named "Dirk" to save their group.

This has uncomfortable implications. The same neurochemical system that makes you love your family and trust your friends also makes you suspicious of strangers and hostile toward perceived enemies. Bonding and bias are two sides of the same coin.

---

## Can We Overcome In-Group Bias?

Sapolsky is cautiously optimistic. While the tendency toward Us/Them categorization is deeply biological, the boundaries of the categories are malleable. Throughout history, the circle of moral concern has expanded — from family to tribe to nation to, in some people, all of humanity. The philosopher Peter Singer, in his 1981 book *The Expanding Circle*, argued that moral reasoning itself tends to expand the scope of our concern: as we generalize our principles, we include more beings within the circle of those who matter. What begins as loyalty to kin can, through reflection and argument, extend to strangers, to other species, and to future generations.

The key mechanism is contact — meaningful, equal-status interaction between groups. Soldiers who fought alongside members of other races in World War II were significantly less prejudiced than those who did not. The Christmas Truce of 1914, which Sapolsky discusses, is a vivid example: British and German soldiers who had been killing each other emerged from their trenches, shared food and cigarettes, and played football. They had to be rotated to different sections of the front because they could no longer bring themselves to fire at people they had come to see as individuals rather than enemies.

## The Neuroscience of Empathy

Empathy is not a single thing. Sapolsky distinguishes between emotional empathy, cognitive empathy, and compassion.

Emotional empathy is feeling what another person feels. It involves mirror neurons and the anterior insula — brain regions that activate both when you experience pain and when you observe someone else experiencing it. But emotional empathy has limits. It can produce personal distress rather than helping behavior, and it is biased toward people who look like you, belong to your group, or whom you like.

Cognitive empathy is more deliberate and less biased. It is the capacity to take someone else's perspective — to understand their situation and motivations without necessarily feeling their feelings. Psychopaths, interestingly, have intact cognitive empathy — they understand what you are feeling — but impaired emotional empathy. They do not care. This combination makes them effective manipulators.

Compassion — the motivation to alleviate suffering — appears to be trainable. Matthieu Ricard, a Buddhist monk, has been studied extensively by neuroscientists. When he meditates on compassion, his brain shows patterns of activity that are qualitatively different from, and more sustainable than, simple emotional empathy.

## Cooperation as the Human Superpower

Yuval Harari argues that large-scale cooperation is what sets Homo sapiens apart from every other species. And cooperation, at scale, requires something beyond kin selection and reciprocal altruism. It requires shared stories — narratives that bind strangers together and coordinate their behavior.

Money is the most universal of these stories. Two people who share no language, no culture, and no religion can cooperate through trade because both believe in the same pieces of paper or digital entries. Religion, nationalism, and ideology serve similar functions, binding large groups through shared belief.

The dark side, as Harari notes, is that stories do not feel. Nations do not suffer. Corporations do not grieve. Only conscious beings do. The tragedy of history is that we often serve our stories rather than using them to reduce suffering. Wars are fought for flags, economies are built on assumptions, and millions suffer for abstractions that exist only in collective imagination.

Nassim Nicholas Taleb, the Lebanese-American scholar of risk and uncertainty, adds a powerful moral principle to the discussion of cooperation: skin in the game. Taleb argues that sustainable cooperation requires symmetry of risk — the people who make decisions must bear the consequences of those decisions. When decision-makers are insulated from the costs of being wrong — when bankers collect bonuses during booms but are bailed out during busts, when politicians start wars but do not fight in them, when executives earn huge bonuses while their companies collapse — the incentive structure is fundamentally broken. Risk is transferred from those who understand it to those who do not, and the result is systemic fragility.

Skin in the game is not just a principle of good governance; Taleb argues it is the oldest moral intuition humans have. The Code of Hammurabi — one of the earliest known legal codes, from ancient Babylon — specified that if a builder constructs a house and the house collapses and kills the owner, the builder shall be put to death. This is extreme, but the principle is clear: you bear the consequences of your actions. When this principle is violated — when people can profit from risk without bearing the downside — corruption, recklessness, and systemic collapse follow. The 2008 financial crisis was, in Taleb's analysis, a massive case of skin-in-the-game violation: traders and executives who took enormous risks collected enormous bonuses when the bets paid off and faced no consequences when the bets failed, because the losses were absorbed by taxpayers and pensioners who had no say in the decisions.

## The Illusion of Free Will

Sam Harris is direct: free will is an illusion. Thoughts and intentions arise in the brain without conscious authorship. You do not choose your next thought — it appears. You can verify this by sitting quietly and trying to predict what you will think next. You cannot, because "you" are not generating the thoughts; the brain is.

Sapolsky arrives at the same conclusion from biology: every behavior is the product of prior causes — neurons, hormones, genes, development, culture. There is no moment at which a disembodied "you" steps in and makes a free choice independent of all these factors. The feeling of free will is real — it is a genuine experience — but the experience does not correspond to how the brain actually works.

Harris adds a nuance: the illusion of free will is itself an illusion. When you look closely at your experience, you cannot actually find the sense of free will that you thought was there. Thoughts arise on their own, just as sounds arrive at your ears without your choosing them. Meditation — the practice of observing experience closely — reveals this directly.

---

## Moral Responsibility Without Free Will

If free will is an illusion, can we still hold people responsible for their actions? Both Harris and Sapolsky say yes — but the nature of responsibility changes.

We can still protect society from dangerous individuals, just as we quarantine people with contagious diseases. We can still try to change behavior through incentives and consequences. What we cannot do, with intellectual honesty, is claim that a criminal "deserves" to suffer — that retributive punishment is justified by some metaphysical ability to "have done otherwise."

Sapolsky argues that understanding the biological roots of behavior does not require forgiving all behavior. It does require abandoning the concept of "evil" — the idea that some people are simply bad by nature. Everyone's behavior is the product of causes they did not choose. This does not mean we should not intervene. It means we should intervene with understanding rather than vengeance.

Harris puts it this way: you can hold people accountable without pretending they could have done otherwise. Acknowledging that behavior has causes does not prevent you from wanting to change those causes.

Lee Cronin adds a different perspective: free will may require genuine temporal openness — the future must be non-deterministic for novelty to exist. If the future is truly open, then perhaps agency is real in some meaningful sense, even if it does not correspond to the folk intuition of a "soul" making decisions.

## Wild Problems: When Reason Is Not Enough

Russ Roberts — the economist, podcaster (EconTalk), and president of Shalem College in Jerusalem — offers an important counterpoint to the rationalist approach that runs through much of this course. In his book *Wild Problems*, Roberts argues that the most important decisions in life — whether to marry, whether to have children, whether to change careers, whether to move to a new country — are fundamentally different from the kind of problems that reason, data, and cost-benefit analysis can solve.

The problem is not that we lack information. The problem is that these decisions change who you are. You cannot evaluate whether having children will make you happy, because the person who has children is not the same person who is making the decision. The "you" on the other side of the decision has different values, different priorities, different sources of meaning. Darwin himself made a pro-and-con list about whether to marry — his rational analysis concluded he should not — and then married anyway, quite happily. The list could not capture what marriage would turn him into.

Roberts draws on the economist Friedrich Hayek, who warned about what he called "the pretense of knowledge" — the tendency to think we know more than we do, particularly about complex systems. "The curious task of economics," Hayek wrote in his Nobel Prize lecture, "is to demonstrate to men how little they really know about what they imagine they can design." Roberts extends this to life decisions: we imagine we can design our optimal life through analysis and planning, but the most important outcomes emerge from commitments we cannot fully understand in advance.

This has moral implications. If the most consequential decisions resist rational optimization, then the moral life cannot be reduced to a calculation — not even the utilitarian calculation that Harris proposes. It requires something more like courage, faith (in the secular sense of commitment without certainty), and the willingness to be transformed by your choices rather than controlling them.

Roberts makes a distinction between marriage as a contract (an agreement between parties, breakable when terms are violated) and marriage as a covenant (a commitment that transforms both parties and is sustained by the transformation itself). "Until you're married," he says, "you're an idiot" — meaning that the self-centeredness of single life is something you cannot see from within it, just as you cannot see the water you are swimming in. The covenant changes you in ways that the contract does not, and those changes are often the most valuable outcomes of the decision.

The Talmud captures Roberts's view concisely: "It is not up to you to finish the work, but neither are you free to desist from it." You cannot complete the project of living a good life — it is too complex, too uncertain, too dependent on factors beyond your control. But you are not free to stop trying. The obligation is to act, not to optimize.

## Toward a Science of Morality

Harris describes morality as "an undeveloped branch of science." We have the tools — neuroscience, psychology, economics, game theory — to study what increases and decreases human wellbeing. What we lack is the will to apply them without deferring to religious authority, cultural tradition, or philosophical hand-wringing about the is-ought gap.

He compares the current state of moral inquiry to medicine before the germ theory of disease. For centuries, doctors treated illness with bloodletting, prayer, and superstition. The discovery that diseases were caused by microorganisms transformed medicine from a collection of folk remedies into a science. A similar transformation is possible for morality — from cultural inheritance and religious doctrine to a rigorous, evidence-based inquiry into human flourishing.

Science cannot tell you what to value in the same way it cannot tell you what to eat — but it can tell you the consequences of your choices. If you value human flourishing, science can tell you which policies, practices, and institutions promote it and which undermine it. If you value reducing suffering, science can measure suffering and identify its causes.

David Deutsch would add that moral knowledge, like all knowledge, grows through conjecture and criticism. We propose moral frameworks, test them against evidence and argument, and discard the ones that fail. There is no final moral theory, just as there is no final scientific theory. But there is progress — and it is real.

Derek Sivers offers a pragmatist counterpoint that is worth sitting with. In his framework, which he calls "Useful Not True," almost nothing is objectively true — only things so basic that, as he puts it, "a cat and an octopus would agree on them" (such as "my hand is on the table") qualify as factual. Everything else — including moral principles — is interpretation. Beliefs, in Sivers's view, are placebos: they are not true or false but helpful or unhelpful. "When we say 'I believe,'" he argues, "it's an indicator that what we're about to say next is not true." This sounds like nihilism, but Sivers avoids that conclusion by deferring to Harris on objective morality: he treats wellbeing as the standard for evaluating which beliefs are useful.

The tension between Deutsch's realism (moral knowledge can be genuinely true, and we can make progress toward it through explanation) and Sivers's pragmatism (moral beliefs are tools to be chosen for their usefulness) is one of the deepest in this collection. In practice, they often recommend the same actions — be honest, reduce suffering, question authority, remain open to being wrong. But they disagree about whether moral knowledge is something we discover or something we construct. The disagreement may matter less than it seems: both approaches lead to the same practical conclusion that moral inquiry should be ongoing, provisional, and responsive to evidence.

## What We Know So Far

From the convergence of sources in this course, certain moral insights emerge with something approaching scientific confidence.

Suffering is real and objectively bad. This is the bedrock. When a conscious being suffers, that suffering is not a matter of cultural interpretation. It is a state of the brain, and it is the kind of thing that morality should be about.

Cooperation produces better outcomes than conflict. This is supported by evolutionary biology through reciprocal altruism, by game theory through the iterated prisoner's dilemma, by economics through gains from trade, and by history through the long-term trend toward less violence and more cooperation documented by Ridley and others.

In-group bias is a biological default, not a moral guide. The tendency to favor "us" over "them" is powerful, automatic, and deeply wired. But it can be expanded, and the moral arc of history has been toward expanding it.

Free will is an illusion, but responsibility is not. Understanding the causes of behavior allows for more effective — and more compassionate — interventions than retributive punishment.

Religion is not necessary for morality. Secular frameworks grounded in wellbeing, evidence, and honest conversation can produce moral guidance that is more reliable, more humane, and more adaptable than any dogma.
